Magnetic frustration in Na2IrO3

Many solids, such as Iron or Nickel, are said to be magnetic because they have unpaired electrons that interact strongly with one another and tend to align in the same direction--this cooperation between neighboring electrons is the driving force behind a vast array of technologies, from microphones to hard drives. In certain rare materials, however, neighboring electrons refuse to align, despite having strong interactions with one another. This phenomenon, known as "magnetic frustration," is a hot topic among physicists because it has the potential to produce exotic magnetic states which could be harnessed to produce a new generation of magnetism-driven electronics. In this publication we identify specific chemical and structural defects that exist in the frustrated magnetic material Sodium Iridium Oxide, and demonstrate the ways in which these defects destroy the interesting magnetism.
