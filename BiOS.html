Superconductivity in Bismuth Oxy-Sulfides

In the early 1900's, H. K. Onnes discovered that some metals, when cooled to very low temperatures, can conduct electricity with absolutely no resistance. This phenomenon, dubbed "superconductivity," is essential to many of today's cutting-edge technologies, such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging and high speed "mag-lev" trains, but generally only occurs at temperatures very close to absolute zero. Layered materials can superconduct at higher temperatures, making them attractive for future applications, but they are often structurally complex and fragile. In this work, we investigate claims of superconductivity in a new layered material (bismuth oxysulfide) and demonstrate that the reported structure actually contains a mixture of several compounds. We isolate the true superconducting material from this mixture and characterize its structural and physical properties using transmission electron microscopy, X-ray diffraction, and magnetometry.
